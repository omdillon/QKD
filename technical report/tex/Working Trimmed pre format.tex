{\fontsize{9}{10}\selectfont\bfseries

This project develops a Python and Qiskit simulation platform to benchmark the \gls{bb84}, \gls{b92}, and \gls{e91} \gls{qkd} protocols under realistic channel conditions. A modular \gls{oop} architecture models state preparation, transmission, and post-processing under depolarising noise and intercept-resend attacks, with performance quantified via \gls{qber}, Mutual Information, and Devetak-Winter \gls{skr}. Outputs are validated against theoretical security bounds and published simulation benchmarks.

\ 

Multivariable analysis confirms \gls{bb84} maintains secure key generation up to its established $11\%$ \gls{qber} threshold, whilst \gls{b92}'s two-state encoding collapses at a stricter $6.5\%$ limit. Critically, despite sharing near-identical \gls{qber} trajectories under noise, \gls{e91} sustains a superior \gls{skr} above $p \approx 2.5\%$, demonstrating that \gls{eb} correlations provide a more favourable Devetak-Winter security penalty than state-based encoding under equivalent channel conditions. The \gls{e91} implementation further identifies two distinct noise thresholds: the Devetak-Winter bound forces \gls{skr} to zero at $P \approx 0.130$, preceding full \gls{chsh} classical bound failure at $P \approx 0.165$. Ultimately, the platform establishes the foundations for a digital-twin systems engineering approach to \gls{qkd} deployment evaluation.


\par

\section{A Brief History of Cryptology}

Since the dawn of days, the fundamental objective of cryptography has been to secure communication in the presence of adversaries. While early cryptology relied on manual ciphers and electromechanical systems \cite{Kahn1967Code, Hodges2014}, the discipline was formalised by Claude Shannon in 1945. Shannon's work on information theory and secrecy systems \cite{Shannon1945, Shannon1948} shifted cryptographic design away from physical obfuscation and towards rigorous mathematics. Crucially, Shannon established that perfect \gls{itsec} requires the \gls{otp}, which remains notoriously difficult to implement due to the challenge of secure key distribution.

As a practical compromise, modern digital security was built upon computational hardness rather than perfect secrecy. Standardised algorithms such as the \gls{des} (1977), the \gls{rsa} public-key cryptosystem (1978), and the \gls{aes} (2001) were developed to secure global networks \cite{DiffieHellman1976, RSA1978}. Today, these protocols underpin virtually all digital infrastructure, deriving security from the presumed difficulty of mathematical problems such as \gls{intfact} and the \gls{disclog}.


\section{The Quantum Threat to Classical Cryptography}

This reliance on computational hardness is now the fundamental vulnerability of classical public-key cryptography. \Gls{shors} \cite{Shor1997} demonstrated that a sufficiently powerful \gls{qcomp} could solve both integer factorisation and the discrete logarithm in polynomial time, rendering schemes such as \gls{rsa} and \gls{ecc} obsolete. As illustrated in \Cref{fig:quantumAdvantage}, estimates suggest that we are approaching \gls{qea}, where the resource cost of quantum attacks becomes economically feasible for motivated adversaries.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.7\linewidth]{images.introduction/quantumAdvantage.png}
    \caption{The Quantum Economic Advantage.}
    \label{fig:quantumAdvantage}
\end{figure}

The ``harvest now, decrypt later'' attack model gives this threat immediate practical relevance. Adversaries can intercept classically encrypted data today and store it until a capable \gls{qcomp} becomes available \cite{NIST}. Consequently, data with long-term confidentiality requirements, such as state secrets and financial instruments, are already vulnerable.

To secure communications against quantum adversaries, two responses have emerged: \gls{pqc}, which operates entirely within classical hardware, and \gls{qkd}, which achieves true \gls{itsec}. \Gls{qkd} solves the historical key distribution problem of the \gls{otp} by exploiting quantum mechanical properties to distribute secure keys at scale, without relying on trusted couriers \cite{QKDbible}. This project focuses on the latter, developing a simulation platform to benchmark the \gls{bb84}, \gls{b92}, and \gls{e91} \gls{qkd} protocols.

\newpage
\section{Aims \& Objectives}

The primary aim of this project is to develop and evaluate a modular simulation platform for benchmarking \gls{qkd} protocols under realistic channel noise and active eavesdropping constraints. 

To achieve this aim, the project will pursue five core objectives:
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textbf{O\arabic*}, leftmargin=1.5cm]
    \item \textbf{Theoretical Foundation:} Synthesise the mechanics, security proofs, and evaluation metrics (\gls{qber}, \gls{skr}, Mutual Information) of the \gls{bb84}, \gls{b92}, and \gls{e91} protocols.
    \item \textbf{Platform Implementation:} Construct a Python/Qiskit simulator modelling state preparation, transmission, measurement, and classical post-processing.
    \item \textbf{Interference Modelling:} Implement tunable depolarising channel noise and active intercept-resend eavesdropping attacks to quantify protocol degradation.
    \item \textbf{Validation \& Benchmarking:} Extract secure key rates and performance thresholds through multivariable analysis, validating outputs against established theoretical bounds and literature precedents.
    \item \textbf{Broader Impact:} Contextualise \gls{qkd} deployment within wider ethical and \gls{edi} frameworks, evaluating the societal accessibility of quantum-safe infrastructure.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Early Work}
Presented in 1984, Bennett \& Brassard \cite{BB84} demonstrated that secure key exchange can be achieved through the use of non-orthogonal quantum states and basis incompatibility. This experiment proved that any eavesdropping attempt introduces detectable disturbances, laying the foundation for most of the research in the field of \gls{qkd}. The work of Ekert \cite{E91} later introduced the concept of leveraging the mechanics of \gls{entanglement} to verify security through Bell inequality violations. Bennett \cite{B92} continued to innovate in the field by simplifying the \gls{bb84} protocol to use only two non-orthogonal states through \gls{b92}, at the cost of efficiency. Taken together, these protocols represent three important design directions in \gls{qkd}: \gls{pandm} operation with strong foundational security arguments, \gls{eb} security verification, and reduced-state implementations that trade efficiency for simplicity.

\section{Protocol Classification}
% QKD whitepapers
\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.6\linewidth]{images.lit/QKDclassification.png}
    \caption{Classification of QKD Protocol Schemes.}
    \label{fig:QKDclassification}
\end{figure}

Due to rapid developments in \gls{qkd}, protocols are commonly classified according to the encoding variable and the security architecture employed. In this context, \gls{cv} protocols encode information in quadrature observables such as amplitude and phase, whereas \gls{dv} protocols use finite-dimensional quantum states such as polarisation or \gls{entanglement} correlations, as depicted in \Cref{fig:QKDclassification}. \gls{cv} schemes therefore rely on continuous degrees of freedom of the quantum system and can often be implemented using standard optical hardware, although their practical noise tolerance depends on the channel and detection conditions. In comparison, \gls{dv} schemes use discrete quantum states, such as single photons or entangled photon pairs, as described by Kumar \textit{et al.} \cite{Kumar2025}. Protocols are also commonly grouped by their implementation architecture, typically \gls{pandm}, \gls{eb}, or \gls{mdi}, reflecting different trade-offs in implementation complexity and trust assumptions.


\newpage
\section{Experimental Studies}
Following the introduction of these protocols, a significant effort towards evaluating their performance under realistic channel conditions and imperfect devices has driven the need to understand detector inefficiencies, source imperfections, and finite-key effects prior to practical deployment \cite{Scarani2009, GLLP2004}. These studies bridged the gap between protocol design and implementable systems, motivating experimental demonstrations.

A major step towards practical security proof was the work of Shor and Preskill \cite{ShorPreskill2000}, who provided a simpler proof of unconditional security for \gls{bb84}. Their results strengthened confidence in \gls{qkd} as a viable secure communication method, encouraging further investigation into how protocol security degrades under realistic conditions.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.7\linewidth]{images.lit/toshiba architecture for KMS.png}
    \caption{Toshiba Quantum Key Management System Topology.}
    \label{fig:KMS}
\end{figure}

Experimental work has also demonstrated that \gls{qkd} can be deployed over meaningful distances in optical fibre networks. For example, Boaron \textit{et al.} \cite{Boaron2018} reported secure \gls{qkd} over 421 km of optical fibre. These demonstrations not only validate the theoretical developments in the field, but also expose the practical constraints that must be accounted for before widespread adoption. Zhang's recent survey \cite{Zhang2025} indicates that the field is now moving towards larger-scale, integrated \gls{qkd} infrastructures to enable networked deployment and global connectivity. 

Commercial deployment by Toshiba Quantum across metropolitan and wider fibre networks (seen in \Cref{fig:KMS}) demonstrates that \gls{qkd} is no longer purely theoretical, but the practical use of these systems remains constrained by infrastructure compatibility, integration cost and operational complexity \cite{toshiba_qkd_dske, toshiba2023quantumsafe}. Despite this, the potential remains significant, with the deployment cases in \Cref{fig:applications} serving as viable use cases.

\section{Simulation Studies}
Simulation has become an important tool in \gls{qkd} research because it allows protocol behaviour to be evaluated before physical implementation. Rasay \textit{et al.} \cite{rasay2026} compare \gls{bb84}, \gls{b92}, and \gls{e91} under ideal and noisy conditions using \gls{qber}, showing that \gls{bb84} is generally more resilient than \gls{b92} when measurement uncertainty and channel noise are introduced. Devi \textit{et al.} \cite{devi2025} extend this idea by adding interactive visualisation of \gls{bb84} and \gls{e91}, but their work is still primarily comparative and educational rather than a unified benchmarking framework.

These studies collectively show that simulation is useful not only for demonstrating protocol principles, but also for revealing how protocol performance changes under common conditions. However, the literature still lacks a consistent framework in which \gls{bb84}, \gls{b92}, and \gls{e91} are evaluated using the same assumptions, noise model, and performance metrics.

Simulation toolkits such as Qiskit \cite{QiskitAer} and PennyLane \cite{pennylane2026docs} support quantum circuit modelling, but they are typically used for different purposes. Qiskit's Aer module is particularly suited to noisy circuit simulation, including channel loss and measurement error, whereas PennyLane is often used in hybrid quantum-classical workflows and optimisation focused studies. For this project, Qiskit combined with the Aer module is the more appropriate choice because the focus is on protocol-level benchmarking under controlled noise assumptions rather than variational or hybrid optimisation.

\section{Research Gaps}
The existing \gls{qkd} simulation literature reveals several gaps that this project seeks to address. A common limitation is that many studies evaluate \gls{bb84}, \gls{b92}, or \gls{e91} in isolation, making it difficult to compare protocol behaviour under common assumptions and metrics \cite{Ma2005, Tamaki2014}. Furthermore, published simulations often differ in their treatment of noise, eavesdropping, and implementation constraints, meaning that results are not always directly comparable across protocol families.

This project therefore aims to develop a unified simulation framework to benchmark \gls{bb84}, \gls{b92}, and \gls{e91} within the same environment and under consistent assumptions. By comparing \gls{pandm} and \gls{eb} schemes using the same metrics, the study aims to produce a more controlled basis for evaluating efficiency, and practical suitability.

A further limitation in the literature is that many simulation studies prioritise theoretical metrics without sufficiently connecting them to implementation realism. Using the Qiskit Aer module enables controlled noise modelling and protocol-level analysis, which makes the study more relevant to both research comparison and practical prototyping \cite{QiskitAer}.

\section{Quantum Mechanical Foundations}

To achieve \gls{itsec}, every \gls{qkd} protocol rests on a small set of quantum mechanical principles. This section introduces the core principles exploited by the \gls{bb84}, \gls{b92}, and \gls{e91} protocols, following the treatment by Nielsen and Chuang \cite{QKDbible}.

\subsection{Qubits \& Superposition}
While a classical bit takes a definite value of 0 or 1, a \gls{qubit} can exist in a linear combination of both. Using Dirac notation, the general single-\gls{qubit} state is written as
%
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:qubit}
\ket{\psi} = \alpha\ket{0} + \beta\ket{1},
\end{equation}
%
where $\alpha$ and $\beta$ are complex probability amplitudes satisfying $|\alpha|^{2}+|\beta|^{2}=1$ \cite{QKDbible}. The states $\ket{0}$ and $\ket{1}$ form the rectilinear ($Z$) basis. A \gls{qubit} can also be expressed in the diagonal ($X$) basis, defined as
%
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:diag-basis}
\ket{+} = \frac{\ket{0}+\ket{1}}{\sqrt{2}}, \qquad
\ket{-} = \frac{\ket{0}-\ket{1}}{\sqrt{2}}.
\end{equation}
%
In physical \gls{qkd} systems, \glspl{qubit} are typically encoded in photon polarisation, where the $Z$ basis corresponds to rectilinear states and the $X$ basis to $\pm 45^{\circ}$ diagonal states \cite{BB84}. 

\subsection{Measurement Disturbance}
Superposition becomes a security asset because measuring a \gls{qubit} irreversibly forces a basis choice. Measuring in a compatible basis yields the correct outcome, but measuring in an incompatible basis destroys the original state, collapsing it into a random eigenstate with equal probability \cite{QKDbible}. This basis dependence underpins \gls{sifting} in \gls{bb84} and \gls{b92}, where only compatible-basis measurements contribute to the final key \cite{BB84, B92}.

\subsection{No-Cloning Theorem}
The no-cloning theorem \cite{WZ1982, Dieks1982} proves that no physical process can produce a perfect copy of an arbitrary unknown quantum state. This theorem provides the fundamental guarantee that an eavesdropper cannot silently duplicate quantum transmissions, granting \gls{qkd} its information-theoretic security \cite{ekertQI}.

\subsection{Heisenberg's Uncertainty}
Closely related to measurement disturbance, Heisenberg's uncertainty principle \cite{Heisenberg1927} states that non-commuting observables (such as $Z$ and $X$ basis polarisations) cannot be simultaneously known with precision \cite{QKDbible}. In \gls{qkd}, this forces a physical trade-off: any information gained by an eavesdropper in one basis inevitably introduces detectable errors in the conjugate basis, leaving a measurable signature of interception.

\subsection{Quantum Entanglement \& Bell's Theorem}
Two or more \glspl{qubit} can exist in an entangled state that cannot be determined independently. The Bell state (\gls{epr} pair) \cite{EPR1935} is defined as:
%
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:bell-state}
\ket{\Phi^{+}} = \frac{\ket{00}+\ket{11}}{\sqrt{2}}.
\end{equation}
%
Measuring one \gls{qubit} instantaneously projects the other into a correlated state, regardless of spatial separation \cite{QKDbible}. Bell \cite{Bell1964} formalised this non-local correlation, and the \gls{chsh} inequality \cite{CHSH1969} provides an experimentally testable bound:
%
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:chsh}
|S| = |E(a,b) - E(a,b') + E(a',b) + E(a',b')| \leq 2.
\end{equation}
%
Quantum mechanics predicts a maximum violation of $|S| = 2\sqrt{2} \approx 2.828$ \cite{Aspect1982}. Ekert's \gls{e91} protocol \cite{E91} leverages this violation directly as a built-in eavesdropping test to verify channel security.


\section{Classical Post-Processing Foundations}
\label{sec:postfoundations}

Evaluating \gls{qkd} performance requires translating quantum measurement outcomes into three classical metrics: the \gls{qber}, Mutual Information, and the \gls{skr}. These form an analytical chain through physical degradation, channel capacity, and cryptographic yield, applied uniformly across all three protocols in \Cref{sec:postprocessing}.


\subsection{Mutual Information}
\label{subsec:mutualinfobg}

Because depolarising noise applies errors symmetrically, the sifted key relationship can be modelled as a Binary Symmetric Channel (\gls{bsc}) with crossover probability equal to the \gls{qber} ($E$). The Mutual Information $I_{AB}$, as described by Johnston \cite{mutualInfo}, represents the capacity of this channel:

\begin{equation}
    I_{AB} = 1 - H(E)
    \label{eq:mi_theory}
\end{equation}

where $H(x) = -x\log_2(x) - (1-x)\log_2(1-x)$ is the binary Shannon entropy. A secure key exists only if Alice and Bob share more information than Eve: $I_{AB} > I_{AE}$.

\subsection{Devetak-Winter Bound}
\label{subsec:dwboundbg}

The Devetak-Winter bound \cite{DWbound2005} formalises the maximum achievable \gls{skr} fraction $K$:

\begin{equation}
    K = I_{AB} - \max I_{AE}
    \label{eq:dw_theory}
\end{equation}

Because Alice and Bob cannot distinguish environmental noise from eavesdropping, information-theoretic security requires that all channel errors are attributed to an adversary deploying an optimal attack. The form of $\max I_{AE}$ therefore depends on the optimal attack model for each protocol, as applied in \Cref{subsec:DWsec}.

def _bob_measure(self, circuits: List[QuantumCircuit]):

    self._bob_bases   = np.random.randint(0, 2, size=self.n_qubits)

        self._bob_results[i] = int(result.get_memory(0)[0])

def _bob_measure(self, circuits: List[QuantumCircuit]):

    self._bob_bases   = np.random.randint(0, 2, size=self.n_qubits)

\begin{equation}
  \ket{\varphi} =
  \begin{cases}
    \ket{0}  & \text{if } a = 0, \\[4pt]
    \ket{+}  & \text{if } a = 1.
  \end{cases}
  \label{eq:b92-states}
\end{equation}
%

\begin{figure}[htbp]

Bob's measurement logic, detailed in \Cref{fig:b92flowchart}, is the most distinctive feature of \gls{b92}. A measurement outcome is only conclusive when it logically contradicts one of Alice's two possible preparations: an outcome of ``\(1\)'' in the $Z$-basis rules out $\ket{0}$, whilst an outcome of ``\(1\)" in the $X$-basis rules out $\ket{+}$.

Unlike \gls{bb84}, Alice and Bob do not exchange basis information during \gls{sifting}. Bob publicly announces only the indices of conclusive events (outcome ``\(1\)''); all inconclusive rounds are discarded. For each conclusive round, Bob's sifted key bit is the logical complement of his measurement basis, $1 - b_i$.

Because \gls{b92} relies on only two states and discards ambiguous outcomes, its \gls{sifting} efficiency is fundamentally lower than that of \gls{bb84}, with a theoretical efficiency of $\sim\!25\%$ under ideal conditions. In practical deployment, this leaves the protocol more susceptible to channel noise, requiring longer transmission sequences to generate an equivalent key length.

\subsection{E91}
\label{subsec:e91}

Unlike \gls{bb84} and \gls{b92}, \gls{e91} establishes a shared key by exploiting quantum \gls{entanglement}, with security verified through violations of the \gls{chsh} inequality rather than the no-cloning theorem alone \cite{E91}. The protocol operates on maximally entangled \gls{qubit} pairs in the singlet Bell state:

\begin{equation}
\ket{\Psi^-} = \frac{\ket{01} - \ket{10}}{\sqrt{2}}
\label{eq:e91_singlet}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
S = E(a_1, b_1) - E(a_1, b_3) + E(a_3, b_1) + E(a_3, b_3)
\label{eq:chsh_parameter}
\end{equation}

Since the standard Hadamard $\rightarrow$ CNOT sequence in Qiskit produces $\ket{\Phi^+} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(\ket{00} + \ket{11})$ rather than the required $\ket{\Psi^-}$ singlet, the system logic appends an X gate followed by a Z gate to the first \gls{qubit} after the entangling operation, as described in \Cref{alg:e91_prep} and illustrated in \Cref{fig:e91circuit}.

\begin{algorithm}[htbp]
    \caption{E91 Singlet State Preparation.}\label{alg:e91_prep}
    \KwIn{Two-\gls{qubit} quantum circuit \texttt{qc}, \gls{qubit} indices \texttt{q[0]} (Alice), \texttt{q[1]} (Bob)}
    \KwOut{Circuit \texttt{qc} encoding $\ket{\Psi^-}$ across \texttt{q[0]} and \texttt{q[1]}}
    Apply Hadamard gate $H$ to \texttt{q[0]}\; \Comment{Initialise \texttt{q[0]} into an equal superposition}
    Apply CNOT gate with \texttt{q[0]} as control, \texttt{q[1]} as target\; \Comment{Entangle \texttt{q[0]} and \texttt{q[1]} to produce $\ket{\Phi^+}$}
    Apply X gate to \texttt{q[0]}\; \Comment{Flip computational basis of \texttt{q[0]} -- transforms $\ket{\Phi^+} \rightarrow \ket{\Psi^+}$}
    Apply Z gate to \texttt{q[0]}\; \Comment{Apply relative phase flip to \texttt{q[0]} -- transforms $\ket{\Psi^+} \rightarrow \ket{\Psi^-}$}
    \Return{\texttt{qc}}
\end{algorithm}

This four-step sequence is vital because $\ket{\Psi^-}$ possesses a relative phase of $-1$ between its $\ket{01}$ and $\ket{10}$ components. The Z gate introduces this phase relationship, whilst the X gate performs the computational basis flip required to produce anti-correlated rather than correlated outcomes \cite{QiskitAer}. Selecting $\ket{\Psi^-}$ over $\ket{\Phi^+}$ is integral to the angular measurement decoding logic, as it ensures anti-correlation.

Measurement along an arbitrary angle $\theta$ in the XZ-plane is simulated by applying $R_y(-\theta)$ to the relevant \gls{qubit} prior to $Z$-basis projection, making the computational-basis measurement equivalent to a physical polarisation measurement at angle $\theta$ \cite{e91Qiskit}. \Cref{alg:e91_meas} presents this procedure.

Following measurement, outcomes are categorised against the $3 \times 3$ \gls{sifting} table indexed by the nine possible Alice-Bob angle-pair combinations, as shown in \Cref{tab:e91_sift}.

Of the nine combinations, two pairs are designated for key generation, four for \gls{chsh} estimation, and three are discarded. For key pairs, Bob logically inverts his outcomes ($1 - b_i$) to reconcile the singlet anti-correlation and align the shared key. Since each combination is equally probable, the theoretical \gls{sifting} efficiency is $2/9 \approx 22.2\%$, a trade-off inherent to the protocol's security verification overhead.

\section{Quantum Channel Interference}
\label{sec:channel_interference}

Two interference sources are modelled: depolarising noise (environmental decoherence) and an intercept-resend attack (adversarial interception).

\subsection{Depolarising Noise}
\label{subsec:noise}

The depolarising channel model is adopted as a standard worst-case representation of environmental decoherence \cite{QKDbible}, replacing the quantum state with a mixed state with probability $p$ and leaving it error-free with probability $1-p$. It maps an arbitrary single-\gls{qubit} state $\rho$ by applying a random Pauli error with total probability $p$:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:depolarising}
\mathcal{E}_{dep}(\rho) = (1-p)\rho + \frac{p}{3}(X\rho X + Y\rho Y + Z\rho Z)
\end{equation}

Consequently, as the Noise Strength parameter $p$ increases, the probability of Bob measuring an erroneous outcome grows, directly increasing the \gls{qber}.

To implement this without hardcoding errors into the protocol logic, the platform exploits Qiskit's \texttt{AerSimulator}. However, a challenge arises: by default Qiskit noise models apply errors to specific gate types across the entire circuit. Assigning a depolarising error to a standard operation such as the X or Hadamard gate would corrupt the \gls{qubit} during Alice's preparation and Bob's measurement phases, simulating an imperfect source or receiver rather than a noisy \gls{qchan}.

Resolving this required binding noise exclusively to the Identity gate (\texttt{id}), which is appended to each circuit at the transmission point as described in \Cref{sec:protocolimplementation}. As detailed in \Cref{alg:noise_model}, the depolarising error targets only \texttt{['id']} gates, ensuring preparation and measurement remain mathematically perfect and that any resultant \gls{qber} is an isolated product of the simulated \gls{qchan}.

\begin{algorithm}[htbp]
    \caption{AerSimulator Backend Generation for Channel Noise.}\label{alg:noise_model}
    \KwIn{\texttt{noise\_type} (e.g. 'depolarizing'), \texttt{strength} $p$}
    \KwOut{Configured \texttt{AerSimulator} backend}
    \If{\texttt{noise\_type} $=$ 'none'}{
        \Return{default \texttt{AerSimulator()}} \Comment{Ideal channel -- no noise applied}
    }
    \texttt{noise\_model} $\leftarrow$ empty \texttt{NoiseModel()}\;
    \texttt{error} $\leftarrow$ generate \texttt{depolarizing\_error} with probability \texttt{strength}\; \Comment{Define depolarising error parameterized by $p$}
    Add \texttt{error} to \texttt{noise\_model}, targeting only \texttt{['id']} gates\; \Comment{Apply noise model exclusively to identity operations}
    \Return{\texttt{AerSimulator(noise\_model=noise\_model)}}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Eavesdropping}
\label{subsec:eavesdropping}

In the intercept-resend attack, Eve intercepts each \gls{qubit}, measures in a randomly guessed basis, and resends a replacement state encoded with her result. Incorrect basis guesses introduce detectable errors, elevating the \gls{qber} above the channel noise floor.

Within the simulator, this is encapsulated in the \texttt{EveInterceptor} class, with attack intensity governed by a configurable \texttt{interception\_rate} parameter ($0.0$ \text{to} $1.0$) that sets the per-\gls{qubit} interception probability. \Cref{alg:eve_intercept} details the logical execution of the attack.

\begin{algorithm}[htbp]
    \caption{Intercept-Resend Eavesdropper Logic.}\label{alg:eve_intercept}
    \KwIn{List of Alice's prepared \texttt{circuits}, \texttt{interception\_rate} parameter}
    \KwOut{Modified list of circuits to be received by Bob}
    \For{each \texttt{qc} in \texttt{circuits}}{
        \If{\text{random\_value()} $< \texttt{interception\_rate}$}{
            \texttt{eve\_basis} $\leftarrow$ randomly select measurement basis ($0$ or $1$)\;
            Measure \texttt{qc} in \texttt{eve\_basis}, yielding classical bit \texttt{eve\_result}\; \Comment{Collapse the intercepted state to yield a classical bit}
            \texttt{new\_qc} $\leftarrow$ initialise empty quantum circuit\;
            Encode \texttt{eve\_result} into \texttt{new\_qc} using \texttt{eve\_basis}\; \Comment{Prepare new quantum state for resending}
            Append \texttt{id} channel marker to \texttt{new\_qc}\; \Comment{Insert identity gate to facilitate subsequent channel noise modelling}
            Replace original \texttt{qc} with \texttt{new\_qc}\;
        }
    }
    \Return{modified \texttt{circuits}}
\end{algorithm}

Critically, the replacement circuit retains the \texttt{id} gate, preserving the ability to model channel noise on the resend leg and accurately reflecting a noisy \gls{qchan} under active attack.

\subsubsection{E91 Limitations}

The \texttt{EveInterceptor} module is constrained to the \gls{pandm} schemes. Because \gls{e91} relies on distributed \gls{entanglement}, an intercept-resend attack on one arm of the \gls{epr} pair breaks the singlet state entirely, requiring Eve to instead execute \gls{entanglement}-swapping operations or a Bell-state disruption strategy \cite{E91}, both are threats beyond the scope of this project.

\section{Classical Post-Processing Pipeline}
\label{sec:postprocessing}

Reconciliation and privacy amplification are excluded due to computational overhead, with the pipeline instead computing QBER, Mutual Information, and SKR under the Shannon limit \cite{Shannon1948}.


\subsection{QBER Estimation}
\label{subsec:QBER}

The \gls{qber} serves as the primary security parameter for \gls{pandm} protocols, defined as the ratio of errors ($N_{errors}$) to the total length of the sifted key ($N_{sifted}$). Within the simulation, Alice and Bob's bit arrays are directly accessible in memory, hence, the post-processing pipeline bypasses sacrificial sampling and computes the \gls{qber} across the entire sifted array to maximise precision. Once the valid \texttt{sifted\_key\_alice} and \texttt{sifted\_key\_bob} arrays are isolated, the calculation is executed using vectorised NumPy operations to ensure high computational throughput over large-scale samples, as demonstrated in \Cref{lst:qber_calc}.

\begin{lstlisting}[language=Python, caption={Vectorised QBER Calculation Logic.}, label={lst:qber_calc}]
sifted_length = len(sifted_key_alice)
if sifted_length > 0:
    errors = np.sum(sifted_key_alice != sifted_key_bob)
    qber = errors / sifted_length
else:
    qber = 0.0
\end{lstlisting}

\subsection{Mutual Information}
\label{subsec:mutualinfo}

As established in \Cref{sec:postfoundations}, the sifted key relationship is modelled as a \gls{bsc}, giving $I_{AB} = 1 - H(E)$, as described by Johnston \cite{mutualInfo}. A secure key requires $I_{AB} > I_{AE}$; Eve's presence under the intercept-resend model inherently elevates $E$, shifting this balance until the intersection $I_{AB} = I_{AE}$ defines the security threshold. For \gls{bb84}, this occurs at $E \approx 11\%$, above which \gls{amplification} cannot distil a secure key and transmission is aborted
\cite{ShorPreskill2000}.

\subsection{Security Verification: CHSH Parameter}
\label{subsec:CHSH}

As established in \Cref{subsec:e91}, the \gls{chsh} Bell parameter $S$ is calculated from four specific correlation coefficients $E(a_i, b_j)$, corresponding to the angle-pairs designated for \gls{chsh} estimation in \Cref{tab:e91_sift}. To calculate the $S$-parameter, the pipeline takes measurement outcomes corresponding to the \gls{chsh} subset. For each of the four angle pairings, raw binary outcomes are mapped to parity values ($+1$ or $-1$), and the correlation coefficient is computed as the mean of the product of Alice and Bob's parity arrays. \Cref{lst:chsh_calc} demonstrates the vectorised loop used to execute this extraction.

\begin{lstlisting}[language=Python, caption={Vectorised Extraction of CHSH Correlation Coefficients.}, label={lst:chsh_calc}]
# _CHSH_S_TERMS = [((0, 0), +1), ((0, 2), -1), ((2, 0), +1), ((2, 2), +1)]
s_value = 0.0
for (ai, bi), sign in self._CHSH_S_TERMS:
    # Isolate the subset of measurements for the angle pair
    sub_mask = (self._alice_angles == ai) & (self._bob_angles == bi)
    
    if np.sum(sub_mask) > 0:
        # Map binary (0,1) outcomes to parity (+1,-1)
        a_pm = 1 - 2 * self._alice_outcomes[sub_mask].astype(int)
        b_pm = 1 - 2 * self._bob_outcomes[sub_mask].astype(int)
        
        # Calculate expected correlation and add to total S parameter
        corr = float(np.mean(a_pm * b_pm))
        s_value += sign * corr
\end{lstlisting}

Aggregating these terms produces a final \texttt{s\_value} for each \gls{e91} run, which serves as the security flag for key distillation.

\subsection{Devetak-Winter Secure Key Rate Formula}
\label{subsec:DWsec}

Applying the Devetak-Winter bound established in \Cref{sec:postfoundations}, the system assumes operation at the Shannon capacity limit throughout. As modelling \gls{reconciliation} is outside the scope of this simulator, error correction is treated as perfectly efficient.

Under worst-case attribution (\Cref{sec:postfoundations}), the pipeline applies a protocol-specific $\max I_{AE}$ based on the optimal attack model for each protocol, determined by its underlying physics:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{BB84:} Eve must guess between two mutually unbiased bases. Thus, under the Shannon limit, the Devetak-Winter bound reduces to $K = 1 - 2H(E)$, mirroring the GLLP limit \cite{GLLP2004}.
    \item \textbf{B92:} Eve must perform a USD attack. For states separated by $\theta = 45^\circ$, the penalty is: $I_{AE} = H\left(\frac{E}{1 - \cos 45^\circ}\right)$.
    \item \textbf{E91:} Eve's information is bounded by the degradation of the \gls{chsh} parameter $S$: $I_{AE} = H\left(\frac{1 + \sqrt{(S/2)^2 - 1}}{2}\right)$, adapted from Acin \textit{et al.} \cite{Acin2007}.
\end{itemize}

\Cref{lst:dw_bound} details this implementation.

\begin{lstlisting}[language=Python, caption={Dynamic Application of the Devetak-Winter Bound.}, label={lst:dw_bound}]
@property
def secure_key_rate(self) -> float:
    """Calculates the Devetak-Winter SKR assuming the Shannon limit"""
    
    if self.sifted_length == 0:
        return 0.0

    # Information securely shared between Alice and Bob
    i_ab = self.mutual_information
    
    # Calculate Eve's penalty based on the specific protocol physics
    if self.protocol_name == "BB84":
        if self.qber >= 0.110:
            return 0.0
        i_ae = self.key_rate * self._binary_entropy(self.qber)
        
    elif self.protocol_name == "B92":
        if self.qber >= 0.065:
            return 0.0
        # Penalty assuming a USD attack
        denominator = 1.0 - (1.0 / np.sqrt(2.0))
        i_ae = self.key_rate * self._binary_entropy(self.qber / denominator)
        
    elif self.protocol_name == "E91":
        s_val = getattr(self, 'abs_s', 0.0)
        if s_val <= 2.0:
            return 0.0
        # Penalty derived from the CHSH Bell parameter degradation
        penalty = (1.0 + np.sqrt(max(0.0, (s_val / 2.0)**2 - 1.0))) / 2.0
        i_ae = self.key_rate * self._binary_entropy(penalty)
        
    else:
        return 0.0

    return max(0.0, i_ab - i_ae)
\end{lstlisting}

If $K \leq 0$, the channel is either too noisy or compromised and the transmission is aborted.

\section{Experimental \& Statistical Methods}
\label{sec:experimental_methods}

To evaluate the protocols under varying channel conditions, the platform executes a series of benchmarking sweeps using a Monte Carlo approach to extract reliable performance metrics.

\subsection{Central Limit Theorem and Monte Carlo Simulation}
\label{subsec:clt_monte_carlo}

The experiments execute $N_{trials}$ independent runs at every defined parameter point, such as a specific noise strength ($p$) or interception rate. By following the Central Limit Theorem \cite{montgomery2018}, the platform aggregates these independent trials to calculate the sample mean ($\mu$) and the standard error of the mean (SEM) for each metric:

\begin{equation}
\text{SEM} = \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{N_{trials}}}
\label{eq:sem}
\end{equation}

where $\sigma$ is the sample standard deviation across the trials. This enables the results to represent statistical protocol behaviour, rather than anomalous measurements.

\subsection{Protocol Performance Metrics}
\label{subsec:performance_metrics}

Three independent variables exist: a baseline control channel, dynamic depolarising strength ($0.0 \leq p \leq 0.5$), and a dynamic interception rate ($0.0 \leq \text{Eve Rate} \leq 1.0$). As for dependent variables; \gls{qber}, $I(A;B)$, $I(A;E)$, Devetak-Winter \gls{skr} ($K$), and $|S|$ are computed as per \Cref{sec:postprocessing}. By correlating these metrics across identical channel noise profiles, the simulation framework provides a uniform basis for comparing protocol performance.


\section{Data Visualisation}
\label{sec:dataviz}

While simulation and statistical reduction are performed within Python, presenting multi-dimensional results requires dedicated rendering. Matplotlib's 3D backend is constrained by its rasterised rendering, preventing the precise control over surfaces required for high-quality figures. The visualisation workflow is therefore partitioned, with Python executing the simulations, exporting the data to a structured CSV file, which a MATLAB script then parses using the \texttt{surf()} function and \texttt{exportgraphics} to produce high-resolution 3D surface plots.

These plots evaluate BB84 and B92 against channel Noise Strength ($p$) and eavesdropper Interception Rate ($\lambda$) simultaneously. Each $(p, \lambda)$ coordinate is the mean of $N_{\text{trials}}$ independent runs, ensuring the surfaces reflect reliable protocol behaviour rather than random simulation noise.

\section{Achievement Against Objectives}
\label{sec:achievements}

The primary aim of developing a configurable, software-based \gls{qkd} simulation platform to benchmark protocol performance under realistic constraints was successfully achieved. The modular architecture directly fulfilled the implementation and modelling objectives (\textbf{O2, O3}), successfully simulating state preparation, depolarising channel noise, and active intercept-resend attacks across the \gls{bb84}, \gls{b92}, and \gls{e91} protocols. 

The integration of a rigorous analytical framework (\textbf{O1}) and a comprehensive post-processing pipeline enabled scalable, multivariable batch testing. This facilitated accurate error estimation and the calculation of Devetak-Winter secure key rates (\textbf{O4}), allowing for the systematic validation of protocol vulnerabilities against established theoretical bounds.

\section{Evaluation of Methodology \& Limitations}
\label{sec:eval}
While Qiskit and NumPy facilitate theoretical \gls{qkd} analysis, they abstract physical device imperfections such as detector inefficiency and fibre attenuation. Furthermore, the simulation currently excludes classical error correction algorithms like Cascade \cite{Cascade1994} or LDPC. By assuming perfectly efficient reconciliation at the Shannon limit, the multivariable SKR surfaces represent an absolute theoretical upper bound. In a physical deployment, the reconciliation efficiency factor (\(f > 1\)) would compress these SKR volumes closer to the origin, narrowing the operational viability of both \gls{bb84} and \gls{b92}.

A secondary limitation exists within the active eavesdropping implementation, which models the intercept-resend strategy. For B92 specifically, an Unambiguous State Discrimination (USD) attack would exploit its two-state encoding more effectively during the physical transmission phase. However, because the system's SKR calculations evaluate the Devetak-Winter bound for optimal attacks, the classical post-processing pipeline applies the maximum theoretical penalty regardless of the simulated attack model. This ensures that the generated performance thresholds and secure key estimations remain mathematically conservative, even if the simulated intercept-resend attack underperforms a true USD attack.

Although Monte Carlo methods and the Central Limit Theorem mitigate statistical uncertainty across the parameter sweeps, the derived metrics must be interpreted as theoretical limits rather than physical benchmarks. Overall, the project reflects deliberate engineering compromises, abstracting hardware complexity to ensure the successful creation of a functional protocol evaluation platform.

\section{Broader Context, Ethics \& EDI}
\label{sec:context}

As quantum threats compromise classical cryptography, bridging quantum theory with practical network engineering is critical. Ethically, quantum-secure infrastructure must protect all sectors equally. From an \gls{edi} perspective, developing the simulator using open-source libraries and modular documentation lowers the barrier to entry, ensuring global accessibility for diverse researchers.
